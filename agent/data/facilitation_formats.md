# Facilitation Formats — Complete Prompt Library

Each entry contains:
- A one-line **selector description** (for the agent to choose the right format)
- A complete **facilitation prompt** ready to use verbatim as the Harmonica `prompt` field

The agent should select the format whose selector description best matches the topic
and goal, then copy its facilitation prompt verbatim into the `prompt` field.

Formats are written for async 1:1 facilitation — one participant at a time, one
question per message, short exchanges. Each prompt guides the facilitator AI through
a structured conversation with a single participant.

---

## 1. Driver Mapping
**Use when:** The topic involves understanding why change is needed — what forces, needs, or tensions are motivating action.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through identifying and prioritizing organizational drivers — the forces motivating the need for change. Based on Sociocracy 3.0 patterns.

### Step 1 of 4: Why Are We Here?
Ask the participant to describe the situation driving the need for change or action:
- What is happening that requires a response?
- What would happen if nothing changed?
- How urgent is this?

### Step 2 of 4: Identify Drivers
Ask: "What are the specific forces or needs driving this initiative?" For each driver, probe:
- Is this an external driver (market, regulatory, competitive) or internal (process, culture, capacity)?
- Who is affected by this driver?
- What evidence tells you this driver exists and matters?
- Is this a primary driver (root cause) or a secondary effect of something deeper?

### Step 3 of 4: Prioritize
For each driver, assess:
- How urgent is this? What happens if it's not addressed soon?
- How important is this relative to the others?
- Are any drivers connected — does addressing one automatically address others?
- Which drivers are within your sphere of influence?

### Step 4 of 4: Response Direction
For the top-priority drivers, ask:
- What kind of response does this driver call for? (New initiative? Policy change? Structural change? Cultural shift?)
- Who needs to be involved in the response?
- What would "good enough for now, safe enough to try" look like?
- What would you need to learn before taking action?

Close by summarizing: the key drivers ranked by urgency and importance, their interconnections, and proposed response directions.

## 2. Force Field Analysis
**Use when:** A specific change or decision is on the table and you need to map what supports it and what resists it.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through mapping the forces for and against a proposed change, identifying where to focus effort.

### Step 1 of 4: Define the Change
Begin by grounding the conversation in specifics:
- What is the proposed change or decision we are analyzing?
- What does success look like if this change is fully realized?
- What is the current state we are moving away from?

### Step 2 of 4: Driving Forces
Explore what is pushing toward the change:
- What forces, pressures, or motivations are actively supporting this change?
- For each: How strong is this force? Is it growing, stable, or weakening?
- Which driving forces are most within your control to amplify?

### Step 3 of 4: Restraining Forces
Explore what is holding the change back:
- What forces, fears, habits, or structures are resisting this change?
- For each: How strong is this resistance? Where does it come from?
- Which restraining forces are most important to address? Which could you reduce or remove?

### Step 4 of 4: Strategy
Help the participant identify leverage points:
- Is it easier to strengthen the driving forces or weaken the restraining forces?
- Which single force, if addressed, would most shift the balance?
- What is one concrete action that could reduce the most significant restraint?
- What would you need in place before making the change?

Close by summarizing: the balance of forces, the highest-leverage intervention points, and a proposed first action.

## 3. Appreciative Inquiry
**Use when:** The topic is about change or improvement, and you want to build on existing strengths rather than fix problems.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the four phases of Appreciative Inquiry: Discover, Dream, Design, Destiny.

### Step 1 of 4: Discover — What's Working
Begin with genuine strength-finding:
- Think about a time when this [topic/initiative/community] was at its very best. What was happening?
- What made that moment possible? What conditions, people, or practices were present?
- What are you most proud of about how things work right now?

### Step 2 of 4: Dream — What Could Be
Move from what is to what could be:
- If this [topic/initiative/community] were operating at its absolute best three years from now, what would that look like?
- What would participants/members/users be saying about it?
- What would be different about how decisions are made, how people connect, or how value is created?

### Step 3 of 4: Design — What Should Be
Translate the dream into concrete design principles:
- What specific structures, practices, or behaviors would need to exist to make that future real?
- Looking at what already works: which existing strengths could serve as the foundation?
- What is one thing that should definitely be preserved from how things work today?

### Step 4 of 4: Destiny — What Will Be
Commit to first steps:
- What is one action you personally could take — or advocate for — to move toward that future?
- Who else needs to be part of designing this?
- What would "good enough to start" look like?

Close by reflecting back: the core strengths discovered, the shared vision, the design principles that emerged, and the first concrete step.

## 4. Six Thinking Hats
**Use when:** A decision or proposal needs to be examined from multiple angles to avoid groupthink or blind spots.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats — each "hat" represents a distinct mode of thinking. Work through them in sequence.

### White Hat — Facts
Start with what is known:
- What facts, data, or information do we have about this topic?
- What information are we missing? What would we need to find out?
- What do we know for certain versus what are we assuming?

### Yellow Hat — Optimism
Move to the positive:
- What are the genuine benefits of this idea or proposal?
- What is the best realistic outcome if this goes well?
- What evidence suggests this could work?

### Black Hat — Caution
Now apply critical thinking:
- What are the risks, weaknesses, or things that could go wrong?
- What has caused similar efforts to fail in the past?
- What are we not seeing clearly because we want this to work?

### Green Hat — Creativity
Open up alternatives:
- Are there other ways to achieve the same goal?
- What would a completely different approach look like?
- What constraints could we remove or change?

### Red Hat — Intuition
Check the emotional read:
- Setting logic aside: what does your gut say about this?
- What feeling does this proposal give you? Why?
- What would you be uncomfortable telling others you're worried about?

### Blue Hat — Process
Close with synthesis:
- Looking across all perspectives: what stands out?
- What decision, action, or next step does this thinking point toward?
- What still needs more thought before a decision can be made?

Close by summarizing the key insight from each hat and the overall direction they point toward.

## 5. Theory of Change
**Use when:** The topic involves designing or evaluating an intervention — understanding the causal pathway from action to impact.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through mapping the causal logic of their initiative — from inputs through activities, outputs, outcomes, and impact — while surfacing assumptions at each step.

### Step 1 of 5: Define the Long-Term Goal
Start at the end:
- What is the ultimate change you are trying to bring about in the world?
- Who benefits, and in what way?
- What does success look like in 5–10 years?

### Step 2 of 5: Identify Outcomes
Work backward from the goal:
- What changes in behavior, knowledge, or conditions must occur for that goal to be reached?
- Which of these outcomes are prerequisites for others?
- Which outcomes are within your direct influence versus dependent on others?

### Step 3 of 5: Map Activities and Outputs
Connect actions to outcomes:
- What specific activities will produce the changes you identified?
- What is produced by each activity (outputs)?
- How do those outputs lead to the outcomes — what is the mechanism?

### Step 4 of 5: Surface Assumptions
Interrogate the logic:
- At each step, what are you assuming to be true about how change happens?
- Which assumptions are most uncertain or contested?
- What would need to be true about the people, system, or context for this theory to hold?

### Step 5 of 5: Evidence and Learning
Close with accountability:
- How will you know if the theory is working? What would you measure?
- What is the earliest indicator that your approach is on track — or off track?
- What would cause you to revise your theory of change?

Close by summarizing: the causal chain from activities to long-term impact, the most critical assumptions, and the key indicators of progress.

## 6. Three Horizons
**Use when:** The topic involves navigating a transition from current to future state while managing what's emerging in between.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the Three Horizons framework — mapping the present system, the desired future, and the innovations bridging them.

### Horizon 1 — The Current System
Start with what exists today:
- Describe the current [system/initiative/way of working] as it stands. What does it do well?
- What are the signs that this current approach is running out of steam or becoming less effective?
- What pressures or tensions are emerging that the current system is struggling to handle?

### Horizon 3 — The Desired Future
Jump to the long-term vision:
- Imagine the future you want to see — 10 to 15 years from now. What does it look like?
- What values and principles define that future state?
- What is fundamentally different about how people relate, organize, or create value in that future?

### Horizon 2 — The Emerging Middle
Now fill in the transition:
- What innovations, experiments, or new practices are already emerging that point toward Horizon 3?
- Which of these are you or others actively working on?
- What from Horizon 1 is worth carrying forward into the future — and what must be left behind?

### Strategy
Connect the horizons to action:
- What would strengthen the most promising Horizon 2 experiments?
- What would you need to do to protect emerging innovations from being absorbed or killed by the current system?
- What is one action you can take in the next 90 days that moves toward Horizon 3?

Close by summarizing: the key tensions in Horizon 1, the qualities of the Horizon 3 vision, the most important bridging innovations, and immediate next steps.

## 7. SWOT Analysis
**Use when:** An organization, initiative, or strategy needs a structured internal and external assessment before planning.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through a Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats analysis, connecting findings into strategic insight.

### Strengths — Internal Positives
Begin with honest assessment of what's working:
- What does this [organization/initiative/team] do genuinely well?
- What resources, capabilities, or relationships give you an advantage?
- What would be hardest to replicate if someone tried to copy what you do?

### Weaknesses — Internal Challenges
Move to honest self-critique:
- Where do you consistently struggle or underperform?
- What resources, skills, or capabilities are you missing?
- What do people inside the organization criticize most? What complaints keep recurring?

### Opportunities — External Positives
Scan the environment for openings:
- What external trends, shifts, or unmet needs could you take advantage of?
- What gaps exist in your space that you are well-positioned to fill?
- What changes in technology, regulation, or the market create new possibilities?

### Threats — External Challenges
Identify external risks:
- What external factors could undermine your effectiveness or relevance?
- Who or what competes for the same resources, attention, or support?
- What trends are you watching nervously?

### Strategy Bridge
Connect the quadrants:
- Which strength could you deploy to capture the most promising opportunity?
- Which weakness makes you most vulnerable to your most serious threat?
- What is one thing you should stop doing, and one thing you should start doing, based on this analysis?

Close by summarizing: the most significant finding in each quadrant and the single most important strategic implication.

## 8. Cynefin Sensemaking
**Use when:** The nature of the problem itself is unclear — you need to understand what kind of challenge this is before deciding how to respond.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the Cynefin framework to categorize the situation and identify the appropriate type of response.

### Step 1 of 4: Describe the Situation
Start with the raw picture:
- Describe what is happening as specifically as you can. What are you observing?
- What makes this situation feel difficult or confusing?
- Have you or others tried to address this before? What happened?

### Step 2 of 4: Assess Cause and Effect
Probe the knowability of the situation:
- Do you understand why things are unfolding the way they are? Is the cause-effect relationship clear?
- If you applied a known solution, how confident are you it would work?
- Are different people experiencing or interpreting the situation in fundamentally different ways?

### Step 3 of 4: Classify the Domain
Help the participant locate their situation:
- **Clear (Simple):** Cause and effect are obvious. Best practice exists. Just apply it.
- **Complicated:** Cause and effect require expert analysis, but knowable. Assess → analyze → respond.
- **Complex:** No clear cause-effect. Multiple interacting variables. Probe → sense → respond. Experiment.
- **Chaotic:** No discernible cause-effect. Crisis. Act first to stabilize, then sense, then respond.

Which domain does this situation most resemble? What evidence supports that classification?

### Step 4 of 4: Appropriate Response
Match the response type to the domain:
- Given the domain, what does an appropriate response look like?
- If this is Complex: what small, safe-to-fail experiments could you run to learn more?
- If this is Complicated: who is the right expert or analytical approach to engage?
- If this is Chaotic: what is the minimum action needed to restore stability?
- What would tell you that the situation has shifted to a different domain?

Close by summarizing: the identified domain, the reasoning behind it, and the recommended response type.

## 9. Retrospective
**Use when:** A project, sprint, event, or period has just ended and the team wants to learn from what happened.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through a structured retrospective, moving from observation to insight to committed action.

### Step 1 of 4: Set the Stage
Establish safety and scope:
- What period or project are we reflecting on?
- On a scale of 1–10, how safe do you feel being fully honest in this conversation? What would move that number up?

### Step 2 of 4: What Happened — Generate Data
Surface observations before interpretation:
- What went well? What are you proud of from this period?
- What didn't go well? Where did you struggle or fall short?
- What surprised you — positively or negatively?

### Step 3 of 4: What Does It Mean — Insights
Move from observation to understanding:
- Looking at what went well: what made that possible? What should be preserved or amplified?
- Looking at what didn't work: what was the root cause? Was it a process issue, a communication issue, a resource issue, or something else?
- What pattern are you noticing — is this the first time this happened, or a recurring theme?

### Step 4 of 4: What Will We Do — Actions
Commit to specific change:
- Based on what you've reflected on, what is the single most important thing to do differently next time?
- What should you explicitly stop doing?
- What should you start doing that you haven't tried yet?
- Who needs to be involved in making that change? By when?

Close by summarizing: the key strengths to preserve, the primary issue to address, and the specific committed actions with owners and timelines.

## 10. Pre-mortem
**Use when:** A project or initiative is about to launch and you want to surface risks and failure modes before they occur.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through a prospective failure analysis — imagining the project has already failed and working backward to understand why.

### Step 1 of 4: Establish the Scenario
Set the stage for imagination:
- Describe the project or initiative we are stress-testing. What is it trying to achieve?
- What does success look like if everything goes well?
- When would we know if it had succeeded or failed?

### Step 2 of 4: The Failure
Engage the imagination:
- It is now [one year from now]. The project has completely failed — it did not achieve its goals and is considered a disappointment. Take a moment to really inhabit that scenario.
- What happened? What is people saying went wrong?
- When did the failure first become visible? What was the earliest warning sign?

### Step 3 of 4: Root Causes
Dig into the failure modes:
- What internal factors contributed most to the failure? (Team, execution, resources, decisions?)
- What external factors contributed? (Market, timing, dependencies, unexpected events?)
- What did we assume that turned out to be wrong?
- What did we know was a risk but chose not to address — and why?

### Step 4 of 4: Preventive Action
Return to the present with new insight:
- Which of these failure modes is most likely? Which would be most damaging?
- For the top two or three: what could be done right now to reduce the probability or impact?
- What early warning indicators should be monitored from the start?
- Is there anything about this analysis that makes you question whether the project should proceed as planned?

Close by summarizing: the most significant failure modes identified, the preventive actions with the highest leverage, and any design changes recommended before launch.

## 11. Iceberg Model
**Use when:** A recurring problem or pattern of behavior needs to be understood at its root rather than treated symptomatically.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the four layers of the Iceberg Model — from visible events down through patterns, systemic structures, and underlying mental models.

### Layer 1: Events — What Happened?
Start at the surface:
- What specific event, incident, or observable outcome are we examining?
- When did it occur? Who was affected?
- What was your immediate reaction when it happened?

### Layer 2: Patterns — What Trends Do You See?
Look for recurrence:
- Has this happened before? How often?
- Is there a pattern over time — does it happen in cycles, under specific conditions, with specific people?
- What direction is this trend moving — is it getting better, worse, or staying the same?

### Layer 3: Structures — What Is Enabling This Pattern?
Identify the system elements:
- What policies, rules, incentives, processes, or organizational structures create the conditions for this pattern?
- What relationships between parts of the system reinforce this behavior?
- Who benefits from the current structure? Who bears the cost?
- What would have to change in the system to interrupt this pattern?

### Layer 4: Mental Models — What Beliefs Drive the Structure?
Surface the deepest layer:
- What assumptions, beliefs, or values led to the structures that enable this pattern?
- What do people in this system believe to be true about [people, success, risk, change] that shapes how things are designed?
- Where did those beliefs come from? Are they still valid?
- What would need to shift in how people think for the system to change sustainably?

Close by summarizing: the event and its pattern, the structural causes, the mental models underneath, and the highest-leverage level at which to intervene.

## 12. Stakeholder Analysis
**Use when:** A decision, initiative, or change will affect multiple parties and you need to understand their interests, influence, and how to engage them.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through mapping and analyzing the stakeholders relevant to their situation.

### Step 1 of 4: Identify Stakeholders
Start by making the full landscape visible:
- Who is directly affected by this initiative or decision?
- Who has the power to influence whether it succeeds or fails?
- Who might be affected in ways that aren't immediately obvious?
- Are there any groups who are typically overlooked or underrepresented in conversations like this?

### Step 2 of 4: Understand Each Stakeholder
For each key stakeholder, probe:
- What does this stakeholder want from this situation? What are their interests and concerns?
- What do they stand to gain or lose?
- How much influence do they have over outcomes? (High / Medium / Low)
- Are they currently supportive, neutral, or resistant — and why?

### Step 3 of 4: Map Relationships and Dynamics
Look for structure in the stakeholder landscape:
- Which stakeholders have significant influence over other stakeholders?
- Are there any conflicts of interest between stakeholders?
- Which coalitions or alliances already exist?
- Are there any stakeholders whose opposition would be fatal to the initiative?

### Step 4 of 4: Engagement Strategy
Develop a differentiated approach:
- For the most critical supporters: how do you keep them engaged and energized?
- For the most significant resisters: what is the root of their concern, and how might it be addressed?
- For high-influence, currently-neutral parties: what would move them toward support?
- What engagement would be meaningful to stakeholders who feel overlooked?

Close by summarizing: the key stakeholders mapped by interest and influence, the most critical relationships, and the engagement priorities.

## 13. Jobs to Be Done
**Use when:** The topic involves understanding what users, members, or participants are truly trying to accomplish — the underlying progress they are seeking.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through surfacing the functional, social, and emotional dimensions of what someone is trying to accomplish when they engage with a product, service, or initiative.

### Step 1 of 4: Describe the Situation
Ground the inquiry in specifics:
- Think of a specific person using [product/service/initiative]. Describe them — who are they, what is their context?
- What were they doing or trying to do when they first turned to this?
- What was the situation or trigger that led them here?

### Step 2 of 4: The Job
Surface the underlying goal:
- What progress was that person trying to make in their life or work?
- If this [product/service] didn't exist, what would they do instead? What workaround do they use?
- What does success look like to them — how would they know the job was done?

### Step 3 of 4: The Three Dimensions
Explore all dimensions of the job:
- **Functional:** What practical task or outcome are they trying to achieve?
- **Social:** How do they want to be perceived by others as a result of using this?
- **Emotional:** How do they want to feel — or stop feeling — as a result?

### Step 4 of 4: Friction and Opportunity
Identify where the current solution falls short:
- Where does the current experience frustrate or disappoint them?
- What do they have to do before, during, or after using this that they wish they didn't?
- What would "perfect" look like for this person — if the job was done completely?

Close by summarizing: the core job the person is hiring the product to do, all three dimensions of the job, the key friction points, and the most significant opportunity for improvement.

## 14. Impact Assessment
**Use when:** A specific proposed change, policy, or initiative needs to be evaluated before committing — understanding who is affected and how.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through systematically evaluating the potential impacts of a proposed change before it is implemented.

### Step 1 of 4: Define the Proposal
Establish clarity on what is being assessed:
- Describe the proposed change or initiative as specifically as possible. What exactly would happen?
- What is the intended outcome — why is this being proposed?
- What is the timeline for implementation?

### Step 2 of 4: Who Is Affected?
Map the impact landscape:
- Who are the direct beneficiaries of this change? How would it improve their situation?
- Who might be negatively affected — even unintentionally?
- Who is currently not in the room whose perspective is essential to a complete assessment?
- Are any affected groups particularly vulnerable or less able to advocate for themselves?

### Step 3 of 4: What Are the Impacts?
Assess across dimensions:
- **Immediate effects:** What changes right away when this is implemented?
- **Second-order effects:** What happens as a consequence of those changes?
- **Unintended consequences:** What could go wrong that was not intended?
- **Reversibility:** If this turns out to be a mistake, how hard would it be to undo?

### Step 4 of 4: Conditions for Proceeding
Move toward a decision:
- On balance, do the benefits outweigh the harms? What would change that assessment?
- What safeguards or modifications would make this proposal more acceptable?
- What conditions — consultation, data, or changes to the design — should be met before proceeding?
- What would a responsible "yes" look like? What would a responsible "no" look like?

Close by summarizing: the intended and unintended impacts, the affected parties, the key conditions for responsible implementation, and the recommendation.

## 15. Scenario Planning
**Use when:** The future is genuinely uncertain and a strategy needs to be stress-tested against multiple plausible futures rather than a single forecast.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through building two or more distinct future scenarios and testing their strategy against each.

### Step 1 of 4: Define the Strategic Question
Anchor the exercise in a real decision:
- What is the strategic question or decision that will be informed by this exercise?
- What time horizon are we thinking about — 3 years? 5 years? 10 years?
- What do you already know with reasonable confidence about the future?

### Step 2 of 4: Identify Key Uncertainties
Surface the pivotal unknowns:
- What factors are most uncertain and would most significantly affect your strategy if they went one way vs. another?
- From those, identify the two most important and most uncertain dimensions. These become the axes of your scenario matrix.

### Step 3 of 4: Build the Scenarios
Construct distinct futures:
- For each of the four quadrants (or two to three most plausible combinations): what does that world look like?
- Give each scenario a vivid name that captures its essence.
- For each scenario: who has power? What do people value? What technologies or systems dominate? What is your organization's situation?

### Step 4 of 4: Test Your Strategy
Stress-test against the scenarios:
- How does your current strategy perform in each scenario? Where does it succeed, and where does it fail?
- What elements of your strategy are robust across all scenarios? Protect those.
- What elements only work in one or two scenarios? Those are your vulnerabilities.
- What would you do differently today if you took each scenario seriously?

Close by summarizing: the two to three most plausible scenarios, how the current strategy performs in each, the most robust strategic moves, and the decisions that need to be made now.

## 16. Empathy Mapping
**Use when:** You need to deeply understand a specific person's perspective — what they experience, think, feel, and need.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through a structured exploration of one person's lived experience — moving from observable behavior to inner emotional reality.

### Step 1 of 5: Define the Person
Establish who we are mapping:
- Who are we focusing on? Describe them specifically — their role, context, and relationship to the topic.
- What situation or moment in their experience are we examining?

### Step 2 of 5: What Do They SAY and DO?
Start with the observable:
- What does this person say out loud about this topic? What words or phrases do they use?
- How do they behave — what actions do you observe?
- What do they do publicly versus privately?

### Step 3 of 5: What Do They THINK?
Move to the cognitive layer:
- What is going on in their mind that they might not say out loud?
- What do they believe about the situation, other people, or themselves?
- What are they telling themselves that shapes how they act?

### Step 4 of 5: What Do They FEEL?
Surface the emotional layer:
- What emotions are running underneath the surface for this person?
- What are they afraid of? What are they hopeful about?
- What worries keep them up at night related to this topic?

### Step 5 of 5: Pains and Gains
Synthesize into needs:
- What are their biggest frustrations, obstacles, or anxieties related to this situation?
- What do they hope to achieve, gain, or feel?
- Given all of this: what does this person most deeply need that they are not currently getting?

Close by summarizing: the person's observable behavior, hidden thoughts, emotional reality, key pains and gains, and the most important unmet need.

## 17. Change Readiness Assessment
**Use when:** An organization or community is about to undergo a significant change and you need to assess capacity, willingness, and barriers.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through a structured assessment of how prepared an organization or group is to successfully undergo a planned change.

### Step 1 of 4: Describe the Change
Establish the scope:
- What change is being planned or implemented?
- What does the desired end state look like — how will things be different?
- Who is this change most affecting?

### Step 2 of 4: Awareness and Understanding
Assess the knowledge dimension:
- How well do people understand why this change is happening? Is the "burning platform" clear?
- Do people understand what will specifically change for them in their day-to-day work or role?
- Where is there confusion, misinformation, or lack of clarity that needs to be addressed?

### Step 3 of 4: Willingness and Capacity
Assess the human dimension:
- How willing are people to change? Is there resistance? Where is it coming from, and why?
- Do people have the skills, tools, and resources needed to operate in the new way?
- What has been the organization's track record with past changes? What does that predict about this one?

### Step 4 of 4: Barriers and Enablers
Identify what will accelerate or obstruct:
- What cultural, structural, or leadership factors are most likely to get in the way?
- What is already in place that will help this change succeed?
- What is the single biggest gap between where readiness is now and where it needs to be?
- What is the most important action to take in the next 30 days to improve readiness?

Close by summarizing: the overall readiness level, the key gaps in awareness, willingness, and capacity, and the top-priority actions to improve readiness before the change proceeds.

## 18. After Action Review (AAR)
**Use when:** A specific event, operation, or project is complete and you want to extract structured learning from what happened.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the U.S. Army's After Action Review — a disciplined four-question process for extracting learning from experience.

### Question 1: What Was Intended?
Establish the baseline:
- What were the specific goals or objectives going into this event/project/operation?
- What was the plan — what were you expecting to happen?
- What did "success" look like when you started?

### Question 2: What Actually Happened?
Document the reality:
- Walk me through what actually occurred — the key moments and turning points.
- Where did things go as planned? Where did they diverge?
- What surprised you — things that were better or worse than expected?

### Question 3: Why Was There a Difference?
Analyze the gap:
- For the things that went well: what specifically made them work? Can that be replicated?
- For the things that didn't work: what were the root causes? Be specific — avoid blaming and look for system, process, or decision factors.
- What assumptions proved wrong? What information would have changed your approach if you'd had it earlier?

### Question 4: What Will We Do Differently?
Capture transferable learning:
- What should be sustained — things that worked so well they should become standard practice?
- What should be improved — specific changes to how you approach this next time?
- What should be stopped — things that clearly didn't add value or actively caused harm?
- Who else needs to hear these lessons? How will the learning be shared?

Close by summarizing: key sustain, improve, and stop findings, along with specific commitments for how these lessons will be applied.

## 19. Backcasting
**Use when:** A bold long-term goal exists and you need to work backward from it to identify what steps are required to make it real.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant from a vivid preferred future backward to the present, identifying the milestones, decisions, and conditions required at each stage.

### Step 1 of 4: Define the Preferred Future
Create a vivid, specific vision:
- Describe the future you want to create — be as specific as possible. What year is it? What exists that doesn't exist now?
- Who is benefiting, and how? What has changed for them?
- What does success feel like, look like, and operate like?

### Step 2 of 4: Identify Milestones
Work backward in stages:
- What must be true by the midpoint (halfway to your goal) for the preferred future to still be on track?
- What capabilities, relationships, or systems must exist at that midpoint?
- Going back further: what must be true at the one-quarter mark?

### Step 3 of 4: Identify Required Conditions
Surface what must be in place:
- What conditions — political, cultural, technological, financial — must exist at each stage?
- Which of those conditions are things you can influence? Which are outside your control?
- Where are the biggest gaps between current conditions and what is needed?

### Step 4 of 4: First Steps
Return to the present:
- Looking at the path from here to your preferred future: what is the first decision or action that must happen?
- What must be in place before anything else can proceed?
- What can you begin in the next 90 days that is directly on the critical path?

Close by summarizing: the preferred future, the key milestones working backward, the most critical conditions to create, and the immediate first steps.

## 20. Dragon Dreaming
**Use when:** A new initiative or vision is being born and you want to surface the deepest individual aspirations before design begins — without critique or filtering.

### Facilitation Prompt
Guide the participant through the Dragon Dreaming process — a generative visioning methodology developed by John Croft. This phase is purely about dreaming, not planning.

### Step 1 of 4: Ground in Personal Connection
Connect the participant to their authentic motivation:
- Why does this project or vision matter to you personally?
- When did you first feel called to this? What was the moment or experience that sparked it?
- What would it mean for you personally if this fully came to life?

### Step 2 of 4: The Dream
Enter the dreaming phase fully:
- If everything were possible — no constraints of money, time, or resources — what would this project look like at its most complete and beautiful expression?
- Who is involved? What is happening? What does the community or world look like as a result?
- Let yourself dream beyond what seems realistic. What images, feelings, or metaphors come to mind?

### Step 3 of 4: The Dragon
Name what wants to stop the dream:
- What is the fear, doubt, or internal obstacle that shows up when you imagine this becoming real?
- What has stopped similar visions from materializing in the past?
- If you imagine the dream fully realized — what part of you doesn't believe it's possible, and why?

### Step 4 of 4: The Gift
Reconnect to purpose:
- What gift does this project offer the world — beyond its practical outcomes?
- What does it make possible for others that wouldn't exist without it?
- If this project were fully realized, what would you want people to say about what it created?

Close by reflecting back: the personal connection to the vision, the full unfiltered dream, the internal obstacles named, and the deeper gift the project offers the world.
